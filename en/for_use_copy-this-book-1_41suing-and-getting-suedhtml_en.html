If you want to prohibit someone who has copied your work from doing so, most of the time you don‚Äôt need to sue. First, ask yourself what you want to accomplish. Theoretically, you can achieve the desired outcome by asking the infringer directly. If your copyright has been infringed upon, here are some things you could demand were the infringement proven in court:

  • Stop the reproduction: The other party must cease distribution of the offending reproduction. That means taking it offline and, in the case of a physical item, destroying any remaining stock.
  • Adapt the reproduction to respect your moral rights: If the other party has infringed on your moral rights, by not giving you proper credit or modifying your work in a way that doesn‚Äôt correspond to your artistic vision, you can ask them to adapt subsequent editions.
  • Pay for the work: If the other party did not pay for your work, you can still ask them to do so after the fact.
  • Pay damages: Because the other party did not follow the legitimate route of asking permission before reusing your work, you can ask for damages. You can ask additional damages for infringement on your moral rights.

Heavy stuff. If you believe the infringing artist acted in good faith, you might not want to release the full arsenal. Especially when it comes to the web, a malleable space where intellectual property rights are not rigorously enforced, it could suffice to ask them to remove the material in question, or to properly credit or link back to you.
