Some Copies are Better than Others: Copying and Ethics

There is a schema of copyright critique that points to the fundamental role copying has in art history, shows that copyright gets it wrong when it comes to how creativity works and thereby claims copyright to be useless.

A closer look would have shown, that while copying is indeed a basis of any form of cultural exchange, artists have consistently tried to promote some forms of copying over others. The policing of which forms of copying are acceptable and which aren‚Äôt is done in part by critics and artists themselves (the court of public opinion) and in part by the legal system. These two don‚Äôt overlap completely. There are forms of copying that are legally permitted but frowned upon by artists and vice versa.

Synonyms for copying

Many of the words used for copying carry with them a moral judgment. Cultural appropriation

In recent years the connotations of appropriation have become negative with the prevalence of the notion of cultural appropriation, especially in the United States. If appropriation means to take something that is not yours in order to subvert it, the original, more or less positive understanding of appropriation art only made sense because artists were assumed to be taking a stand against something larger and more powerful than themselves‚Äîcapitalism, colonialism, patriarchy. What the concept of cultural appropriation emphasises is that artists also occupy positions of power for which they should be held accountable.

When it comes to cultural appropriation, copyright law hardly gets involved. This is because copyright only applies to individual artistic expressions. A style, a flow, a motive, an accent cannot be copyrighted. Plus it is easy enough to find images of non-western cultures that are in the public domain. A culture has no legal personhood; a culture cannot sue. And even if it could, it is not clear who could represent it because a culture is rarely clearly delineated.

Plagiarism

Plagiarism is to pass off the work of someone else as your own. In that way, it‚Äôs a synonym for copying with a more negative connotation.

Straightforward cases of plagiarism are also copyright infringements. Taking an article someone else wrote and publishing it under your own name infringes upon both their copyright (because you copied it without their permission) and their moral rights (because you failed to acknowledge their authorship). While the prototypical kind of plagiarism is textual, it works in other media as well; it is plagiarism any time you take a drawing, photo or piece of software code and pass it off as your own.

It‚Äôs interesting to look at the differences between appropriation and plagiarism. From a legal standpoint, it‚Äôs hard to tell the difference. An appropriation, in the empowering sense, acknowledges the copying, whereas plagiarism hides it. But an artist who appropriates might not explicitly credit the appropriated image, leaving it up to the public to recognise the appropriation as such. Someone called out as a plagiarist may very well defend their work as an appropriation. If we look at the definitions of plagiarism as defined by academic institutions, they tend to have a scope which is quite broad, broader than that of copyright. The requirements for an academic text, then, are much tighter than for a blog post or a newspaper column. The question is, should artists or designers hold themselves to a similar standard? To copy today

If there ever existed a time when artists and designers could copy freely, that time isn‚Äôt now. It‚Äôs not just that many of the favoured tools at artists‚Äô disposal, like sampling, remix, collage or appropriation, are not quite compatible with copyright. It‚Äôs also that the copying faces the scrutiny of your peers, who probably do not dismiss artistic reuse as such but who might get picky over the details. Vallen calls out Fairey and Semaan calls out Serre not because they copy but because of the way they do it.

There are several reasons that, unlike legal restrictions on artistic creation, discussions around plagiarism and cultural appropriation are a great thing for creatives around the world. Firstly, these discussions make clear the game is no longer played by one set of rules because there‚Äôs not one group who gets to set them. As art and design make new connections to activism and technology, new conceptions of art arise and people who might not have had a voice before are heard.

Secondly, as an artist or designer, being open about your influences and references helps to put in perspective the ideology upon which copyright is built. If copyright forbids many of the strategies upon which you‚Äôve come to rely, it‚Äôs only because it is based upon an unrealistic view of creativity, where one creates in splendid isolation. If you can help others to have a more grounded and realistic idea about what it is to create and copy, then that is a great way to help the discussions about copyright move forward.
